中国哲学与精神 Chinese Philosophy & Spirituality

The presence of religions, spiritual beliefs and diverse philosophies is highly prominent in China. Chinese society has a unique blend of many religions embedded in every corner and it's one of the most evident characteristics of the country. Their place in the Chinese society has a huge importance as these philosophies and religions, they not only influenced spirituality, but also government, science, the arts, and social structure. Unlike Western traditions, where religion and philosophy are often distinct, Chinese thought tends to blend them into a unique view of life.

儒家 Confucianism

Confucianism is often characterized as a system of social and ethical philosophy rather than a religion. In fact, Confucianism built on an ancient religious foundation to establish the social values, institutions, and transcendent ideals of traditional Chinese society.

Confucianism was a way of life for ancient Chinese people, and it continues to influence Chinese culture today. The founder of Confucianism, named Confucius, lived from 551 to 479 B.C.E. He was a philosopher and political figure who lived during a time when traditional Chinese principles began to deteriorate under competing political states. He took older religious precepts and translated them into guidelines for social mores. His teachings gave guidance on all levels of ancient Chinese life, from interactions between family members and in the public sphere, to educational standards and how states should be governed. Confucius saw every aspect of life as being made up of obligations between people and entities, and rituals to convey the mutual dependency between them. His teachings focused on humanism, including treating others the way you would want to be treated. He taught that if everyone fulfilled their roles and obligations with respect and kindness towards others, it would build a stronger state. While religious rituals were mentioned alongside all of the other rituals a person was expected to perform, Confucius did not focus on spiritual concerns like the afterlife, gods and goddesses, or mysticism. This is why Confucianism is considered a philosophy rather than a religion, even though it is often lumped in with other major religions.

Confucianism became the dominant political philosophy during the Han Dynasty from 206 B.C.E. to 220 C.E. Because Confucian teachings were conservative and told people to maintain their role in social order, the philosophy was used by the state to keep the status quo from that time forward. The structure of Chinese society and its focus on rituals, familial respect and obligation, worship of ancestors, and self-discipline, remains greatly influenced by Confucius and his teachings.

Core ideas of Confucianism:

  1. 任 Ren (Benevolence): The virtue of kindness and empathy toward others.

    2. Confucianism stresses the importance of social relationships, including those between ruler and subject, parent and child, husband and wife, and friends. Its teachings promote harmony within the family, community, and state.

  2. 李 Li (Ritual): The importance of performing rituals and following traditions to maintain order and respect in society.
  3. 中 Zhong (Loyalty)
  4. 舒 Shu (Reciprocity)
  5. 晓 Xiao (Filial Piety): The duty to respect and care for one’s parents and ancestors.

道教 Taoism

Taoism (also called Daoism) is a Chinese religion that developed a bit after Confucianism, around two thousand years ago. In contrast to Confucianism, Taoism is mainly concerned with the spiritual elements of life, including the nature of the universe. The guiding principle of Taoism is roughly translated as “the Way,” which is a harmonious natural order that arises between humans and the world, and that Taoists should strive to achieve. In the Taoist structure of the universe, humans are meant to accept and yield to the Tao and only do things that are natural and in keeping with the Tao. This is the concept of wu-wei, which translates as “non-action,” but really means to go with the true nature of the world and not strive too hard for desires. This puts Taoism in opposition to Confucianism in another way: it is not concerned about with humanistic morality, government, and society, all of which Taoists see as inventions of humans and not necessarily part of the Tao. At the same time, Taoists were interested in longevity, both of the human body and the soul. Achieving spiritual immortality through becoming one with nature is an important part of the Taoist religion.

Despite their differences, Taoist and Confucian ideas are not completely at odds with each other, so Chinese society was able to absorb concepts from both traditions. Taoism had influence on literature and the arts, but the biggest area of Taoist influence was in science. The Taoist focus on natural elements and observing how the natural world works helped to create Chinese medicine. Similar to the modern scientific method, Taoists observed how different medicines affected people and animals through experimentation. Their collective knowledge gained through trying to improve human longevity made a huge contribution to health sciences.

Core Concepts of Taoism

无畏 Wu Wei

Wu Wei is sometimes translated as non-action, but this wrongly implies that nothing at all gets done. The Tao Te Ching says:

If you try to change it, you will ruin it. Try to hold it, and you will lose it.

道德经 Tao Te Ching

Wu Wei means living by or going along with the true nature of the world - or at least without obstructing the Tao - letting things take their natural course. So Taoists live lives of balance and harmony. They find their way through life in the same way that a river flowing through the countryside finds its natural course.

“A leader is best When people barely know he exists Of a good leader, who talks little, When his work is done, his aim fulfilled, They will say, “We did this ourselves””

道德经 Tao Te Ching

This doesn't stop a person living a proactive life but their activities should fit into the natural pattern of the universe, and therefore need to be completely detached and disinterested and not ego-driven.

道 Dao

The ultimate force that flows through all things, guiding the universe in its natural course. In Chinese, "Dao" means "The Way" or "The Path." It can be understood as the natural, underlying order of the universe. The Dao is formless, eternal, and all-encompassing.

The Dao is often symbolized through nature—like flowing water, which is soft and yielding yet capable of wearing away rock. Nature follows its own course, and humans should do the same, rather than imposing rigid rules or excessive control.

According to Laozi (the founder of Daoism), the Dao cannot be completely captured in words. In the opening line of the Tao Te Ching, Laozi writes: “The Dao that can be spoken of is not the eternal Dao.” This reflects its ineffable nature.

“If you realize that all things change, there is nothing you will try to hold on to. If you are not afraid of dying, there is nothing you cannot achieve.”

Tao Te Ching

阴阳 Yin and Yang

Yin and Yang is a concept that represents the duality and balance of opposing forces in the universe. It is one of the most iconic and influential ideas in Chinese philosophy, deeply rooted in both Taoism and Chinese cosmology.

Yin and Yang are two complementary and interconnected forces that exist in all things. Rather than being opposites that conflict, they are seen as two halves of a whole, constantly interacting and balancing each other.

Yin represents qualities like darkness, passivity, femininity, cold, and receptiveness.

Yang represents qualities like light, activity, masculinity, heat, and assertiveness.

The Yin-Yang symbol (Taijitu) is a circle divided into a black (Yin) and white (Yang) swirl, with a dot of the opposite color in each half. This symbolizes that Yin contains the seed of Yang, and Yang contains the seed of Yin, showing that opposites are interconnected and can transform into each other.

Yin and Yang are not static. They are constantly in motion, growing, diminishing, and transforming. For example, day (Yang) gradually turns into night (Yin), and vice versa. The key to harmony in the universe is the balance between Yin and Yang. Too much of one over the other can lead to imbalance and disharmony. This idea influences Chinese medicine, martial arts, and even government. Yin and Yang are cyclical and constantly shift between dominance and subordination. In Chinese cosmology, this reflects the natural rhythms of the universe—day and night, seasons, life and death. One cannot exist without the other.

佛教 Buddhism

Buddhism was the third major belief system of ancient China. It was founded by Siddhartha Gautama, also called the Buddha, who lived in India around the sixth century B.C.E. Buddhism is a philosophy that focuses on personal development and attainment of deep knowledge. Buddhists seek to achieve enlightenment through meditation, spiritual learning, and practice. They believe in reincarnation and that life is impermanent and full of suffering and uncertainty; the way to find peace is through reaching nirvana, a joyful state beyond human suffering. There are many different sects that place different emphasis on various aspects of Buddhism. The two largest sects are Theravada Buddhism, which is found primarily in southern Asia, and Mahayana Buddhism, which is found in east Asia, including China.

After its founding in India, Buddhism spread to and became popular in China in the first century C.E. Part of the reason Buddhism became popular in China was because of Taoism. Some Buddhist practices were similar to Taoist ones, and Buddhist monks would use Taoist concepts to explain Buddhism to the Chinese, overcoming the cultural and language barrier between Indian and the Chinese people. Buddhism also influenced Taoism with its institutional structure, which Taoists copied and modified. A competition between Buddhism and Taoism arose to gain more followers and greater government influence, and this competition increased the vitality of both religions. As Buddhism became more prevalent, its concepts merged with Taoist and Confucian ideas to become the basis of ancient Chinese society and government. Its influence is seen in Chinese art, architecture, and literature.

Core concepts of Buddhism:

  • 四圣谛 The Four Noble Truths: Life is suffering (dukkha), suffering is caused by desire, suffering can be overcome, and the way to overcome it is through the Eightfold Path (a guide to ethical conduct, mental discipline, and wisdom).
  • 因果与重生 Karma and Rebirth: The belief that actions in this life affect one’s future lives.
  • 涅槃 Nirvana: The ultimate spiritual goal of Buddhism, a state of liberation from the cycle of rebirth and suffering.

佛 Buddha

Buddha, also known as Siddhartha Gautama, was the founder of Buddhism and is one of the most significant spiritual figures in world history. His teachings form the core of Buddhism, a major world religion with a focus on spiritual development, ethical living, and achieving enlightenment.

Siddhartha Gautama was born around the 5th to 6th century BCE in what is now Lumbini, Nepal, to a royal family. His father, King Śuddhodana, was the leader of the Shakya clan, and his mother was Queen Māyā. Siddhartha lived a life of luxury, shielded from the hardships of the world.

After his enlightenment, the Buddha traveled and taught for 45 years. His teachings spread throughout India and eventually across Asia, leading to the development of different schools.

中国民间信仰 Chinese Folk Religion

祖先崇拜 Ancestor Worship

Ancestor worship is a central aspect of Chinese folk religion. It is based on the belief that the spirits of deceased ancestors continue to exist in the afterlife and have the ability to influence the living. The living are responsible for honoring and taking care of their ancestors through offerings, prayers, and rituals.

Families often maintain ancestral altars in their homes, where they make offerings of food, incense, and paper money to ensure that the spirits of their ancestors are content and provide blessings in return.

自然崇拜 Nature Worship

Chinese folk religion also involves reverence for various natural elements, such as the Earth, Heaven, rivers, mountains, sun, moon, and other natural phenomena.

The Dragon is one of the most important symbols in Chinese folk religion, representing power, strength, and control over water and weather. Dragons are associated with rain, rivers, and fertility.

Worship of the Five Elements—wood, fire, earth, metal, and water—is also important. These elements are believed to interact and influence the balance of the universe.

中国神灵 Chinese Deities and Spirits

Chinese folk religion is polytheistic and includes a vast pantheon of gods, goddesses, spirits, and demigods who govern different aspects of the natural world and human life. Some of the most well-known deities include:

  • Mazu: The goddess of the sea, protector of sailors and fishermen.
  • Guanyin: The bodhisattva of compassion, who is worshipped in both Chinese folk religion and Buddhism.
  • Shen Nong: The god of agriculture and medicine.
  • The Jade Emperor: The supreme deity, ruler of heaven and all other gods.
  • Tudi Gong: The local earth god, protector of a specific area or village.

In addition to deities, there is a strong belief in spirits and ghosts. These can be benevolent or malevolent and require proper ritual attention to maintain balance and avoid misfortune.

天命 The Mandate of Heaven

The concept of the Mandate of Heaven is closely tied to traditional Chinese belief systems. It suggests that heaven (Tian) bestows its mandate on a just ruler. If the ruler becomes unjust, corrupt, or tyrannical, they lose the mandate, and the people have the right to rebel and replace them.

F鞥S会 Feng Shui

Feng Shui (meaning "wind and water") is a traditional Chinese practice that seeks to harmonize individuals with their surrounding environment by organizing space in alignment with natural forces. Feng Shui plays an important role in selecting the locations of homes, graves, temples, and businesses to attract good fortune and avoid bad luck.
